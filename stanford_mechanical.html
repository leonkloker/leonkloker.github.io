Graduate Research Assistant

  • Organization: Tang Lab
  • Location: Stanford
  • Duration: January - June, 2024
  • Skills: Machine Learning, Statistical Analysis

I worked as a Graduate Research Assistant in Professor Sindy Tang's lab in Stanford's mechanical engineering department. The research aims to replace traditional exposure-based allergy testing methods by simple blood sampling through the use of machine learning. The blood sample's basophils are analyzed via flow cytometry as they play a significant role in promoting allergic inflammation. The impedance measured by the flow cytometer at various stimulation frequencies after the blood sample has been exposed to an allergen is used to predict the basophil's biomarker activation levels, such as CD63 and Avidin positivity. My role mainly revolved around this prediction task, where a main issues is scarcity of training data. Additionally, the activation levels are given at a cell population level whereas the flow cytometry features are given at the single-cell level. I made use of classical machine learning techniques such as regression models and Support Vector Machines in order to minimize the mean absolute error of the activation prediction.
As the activation of these biomarkers in a stimulated blood sample can be correlated with the allergivity of a patient, the research has the potential to revolutionize the way allergies are diagnosed.
